In Bali, eating is not a simple act of sustenance — it is a ritual. Every dish tells a story of beliefs, seasons and generosity. Balinese cuisine, often overshadowed by its Javanese neighbour or the more widely known Thai food, is in fact one of the most complex and fragrant in Southeast Asia. Here is a guide to discovering it — from morning markets to Ubud's gastronomic tables, all the way to your own kitchen at Villa Mimpi.

**Balinese specialties you must try**

Babi Guling is arguably the island's most iconic dish. This spit-roasted suckling pig, coated in a spice paste — turmeric, galangal, lemongrass, garlic, chilli — is an explosion of smoky, slightly caramelised flavours. Traditionally prepared for religious ceremonies, several warungs now serve it daily. Taste it at least once.

Bebek Betutu is its poultry counterpart: whole duck or chicken, stuffed with a blend of herbs and spices (base genep), wrapped in banana leaves and slow-cooked for hours — sometimes overnight. The meat falls off the bone; the deep, smoky aromas are unforgettable.

Lawar is a ritual salad: finely chopped vegetables, grated coconut, spices and sometimes meat or blood. Dozens of variations exist by region and family. Each bite is a journey into Balinese terroir cooking.

On the soup front, Soto Ayam — chicken broth with aromatics, served with rice vermicelli, lime and kaffir lime leaves — is the perfect comfort food after a morning trek through the rice fields. And for sweet snacking, Jaje Bali, colourful steamed rice cakes, deserve a place in every culinary itinerary.

**The finest gastronomic tables in Ubud**

Locavore — often ranked among Asia's 50 best restaurants — embodies Balinese haute cuisine at its finest. Everything is sourced locally: vegetables from surrounding gardens, fish from the island's shores, spices grown a few kilometres away. The tasting menu changes with the seasons and surprises at every course. Book several weeks in advance.

Mozaic Restaurant Gastronomique, tucked in a romantic tropical garden in the heart of Ubud, offers a Franco-Balinese fusion cuisine of rare elegance. The chef combines classic French techniques with Indonesian produce with remarkable precision. A candlelit dinner here is one of the island's most memorable experiences.

Hujan Locale is the favourite table of connoisseurs seeking refined authenticity. Chef Will Meyrick offers a creative reinterpretation of Indonesian archipelago cuisine — from Sumatra to Papua — with an open terrace overlooking the forest and an impressive list of local cocktails.

Room4Dessert, by chef Will Goldfarb (featured in Netflix's Chef's Table documentary), is an experience unto itself: a menu entirely dedicated to haute cuisine desserts, served in an intimate, poetic setting. Essential for curious epicureans.
